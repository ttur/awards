Worldreader

Creating a World Where Everyone Can Be A Reader

Overview

Worldreader is a non-profit with a big mission: creating a world where anyone can be a reader — forever. In 2013, the global nonprofit introduced a web-based reading solution for mobile phones, built for Worldreader by by Bitville and Futurice. The solution enables millions of readers world-wide to access thousands of free books. The goal was to build a link between Worldreader’s online books and the 350 million people using Opera Mini.

Whether a beginner just learning to read or an adult pursuing a new direction in skills or education, life-long learning is now made more possible with this simple-to-use, platform-agnostic reading solution, available to anyone with an internet-connected phone. Millions of people use this new channel to visit and read, every month. And with features like Read for Children, which coaches parents on how to read to their children for best effect, impacts on literacy rates worldwide are certain to be positively affected.

User interface

Bitville had optimized user interfaces for Opera before, and so had the skills and knowledge to design and optimize user experiences for the Opera browser in this case. Bitville worked with Worldreader to create a cloud-based web app that makes books available so that even people with high latency, low-bandwidth connections can still enjoy reading on their phones.

Content management

The backend, built by Futurice, is a content management system that helps the whole reading experience function smoothly. The system automatically analyzes and pre-processes book information, which is then edited further by the management team. Automatically generated data, such as title and author, is enriched with added categories and defined taxonomies. Books can also be featured in country-specific top lists and collections.

The content management system helps Worldreader editors build context-aware selections that cater to reader's needs in different parts of the world. This helps readers focus on their studies, favorite subjects, or particular authors.

Social impacts

Books are too often scarce. Most people in Sub-Saharan Africa don’t own a single book, and borrowing one from the library can be a real challenge. In Nigeria, for example, there’s just one library for every 1,350,000 people.

According to UNESCO’s survey of 2014, however, 6 out of the 7 billion people on Earth have access to a mobile phone, with cellular networks reaching 95% of the world’s population. Worldreader uses this coverage to bring 28,000-plus free books to 2.5 million users per month in 69 countries. The effect is predictably positive. 62% of Worldreader's reading app users report that they now read more. One-third are reading more to their children, too. And supporting literacy and learning is directly linked to improvements in GDP, no matter the country.
